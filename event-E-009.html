The agitation began as a response to local protests against the repressive policies imposed by the British-backed Tehri Garhwal princely state, which had been granted control over the region under the Treaty of Sugauli in 1815. On May 30, 1930, in a field near Tiladi village in the Rawain Valley, near present-day Barkot in Uttarkashi district, India, unarmed protesters were brutally massacred by the princely state forces. The incident became known as the Tiladi massacre or the Rawain massacre, with estimates suggesting that up to 18 people were killed in the event. This tragic event was part of a broader movement for forest rights and social justice that had been building momentum across India during this period. The aftermath of the Tiladi massacre saw widespread outrage and condemnation from various quarters, including Indian nationalists and local communities who had been affected by the princely state's policies. The incident highlighted the need for greater accountability and transparency in governance, particularly with regard to issues affecting marginalized communities. Today, the memory of the Tiladi massacre serves as a reminder of the importance of protecting forest rights, promoting social justice, and acknowledging the sacrifices made by those who fought for these causes. The legacy of the Tiladi Forest Rights Agitation continues to resonate in Uttarakhand, where efforts are being made to preserve the natural heritage and cultural traditions of the region.
