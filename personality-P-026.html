Born on January 9, 1927, in Maroda village near Tehri, Uttarakhand, Bahuguna showed a strong commitment to social causes from an early age. His dedication to fighting injustice and protecting the environment led him to become one of the most influential figures in Indian environmentalism. The Chipko movement, which translates to "hug the tree" in Hindi, was a grassroots campaign that aimed to protect India's dwindling forests from logging and destruction. Bahuguna, along with his fellow villagers, took on the powerful Tehri Dam project, which threatened to displace entire communities and destroy their livelihoods. The movement's unique strategy involved physical resistance, including women hugging trees to prevent them from being cut down, thereby drawing international attention to the issue. Bahuguna's leadership in the Chipko movement led to a significant shift in India's environmental policies. His tireless efforts resulted in the postponement of the Tehri Dam project, which had been set to displace thousands of people. This victory was a testament to his bravery and the strength of grassroots resistance against powerful corporations. Bahuguna's legacy extends beyond his role in the Chipko movement; he inspired generations of environmentalists and social activists. Throughout his life, Bahuguna received numerous awards for his contributions to environmental conservation, including the Padma Bhushan and the Ramon Magsaysay Award. He also wrote several books on environmentalism and social justice, sharing his insights with a wider audience. Bahuguna's passing on May 21, 2021, marks the end of an era in Indian environmental activism, but his work continues to inspire and motivate new generations of leaders and activists. Despite facing numerous challenges and setbacks throughout his life, Bahuguna remained steadfast in his commitment to protecting India's natural resources.
